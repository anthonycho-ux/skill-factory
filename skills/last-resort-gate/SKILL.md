# /last-resort-gate — Justification Phrase Interrogator

## What It Is

Treat justification phrases as decision gates — not rhetoric. Before committing, each phrase must be backed by specific evidence. If the evidence isn't there, the action isn't justified.

Core principle from Tulsi Gabbard: *"You have to know the enemy that threatens you and why they're doing what they're doing if you have any hope of actually preventing their attack."*

---

## Trigger

Invoke when:
- "Should we do X?" / "Is this justified?" / "Can we skip to action?"
- "Last resort" language is being used to justify a coercive action
- "All options exhausted" is being cited without documentation
- "No other option" appears without a sequenced attempt record
- A commitment's exit condition is undefined

---

## Inputs

- **The justification phrase** — what's being invoked to authorize action
- **Who said it** — their position and institutional incentives
- **What's been documented** — what record exists of alternatives attempted

---

## The Gate Table

Every phrase has a specific gate meaning. If the gate isn't satisfied, the action is not justified.

| Phrase | Gate Requirement |
|--------|-----------------|
| "last resort" | Diplomatic alternatives exhausted, documented, each failed specifically |
| "in our interest" | Direct threat to stated interest — not indirect, not reputational |
| "no other option" | All alternatives tried in sequence with evidence |
| "necessary" | Removing this breaks minimum viable function — not convenience |
| "all options exhausted" | Written record of what was tried and why each failed |
| "proportionate" | Unit cost of first step stated before mission-level cost |

---

## Steps

### 1. Name the specific enemy

Before committing, name:
- The specific actor or group (not "terrorism," "disinformation," "bad actors")
- Their specific capability
- Their specific goal
- Why non-coercive means cannot address them

If the threat is an abstraction → the commitment lacks a specific end state.

### 2. Get the unit cost first

Before the mission cost: what is the unit cost of the smallest component?

*"Every time a soldier walks through the door... that's $35 per head per meal four times a day."*

If you cannot state the first-step unit cost → you have not seriously evaluated the full cost.

### 3. Ask the suppressed question

What is the obvious question nobody is asking?
Who is positioned to ask it?
What institutional price would they pay?

*"Within the institution, in the position of power, to ask the questions like 'Maybe let's not' — it seems really difficult."*

If no one is asking the obvious question → that is the actual risk.

### 4. Define the exit condition first

Before committing:
- What would cause this to end?
- Who decides it's over?
- Is there a point at which continuing is obviously worse than stopping?

If no defined exit condition exists → the commitment has no defined end state.

### 5. Test institutional loyalty vs. integrity

If defending an institution:
- Is it serving its stated purpose?
- Does defending it require defending its current corruption?
- Can it be reformed from inside, or does reform require resignation?

*"I made the decision to resign as vice chair of the DNC so that I could endorse Bernie Sanders."*

If loyalty requires defending corruption → integrity requires resignation.

---

## Success Criteria

- [ ] Specific actor, capability, and goal named
- [ ] Alternatives documented with specific failure reasons
- [ ] Unit cost of first step stated
- [ ] Exit condition defined
- [ ] Suppressed question identified

---

## Rules

- **"Last resort" is usually the first resort.** Unless alternatives are documented, the phrase is rhetoric.
- **Abstractions are not enemies.** "Instability," "the threat," "bad actors" cannot be addressed specifically — they can only be managed.
- **Unit cost before mission cost.** Backwards estimation is not evaluation.
- **Civil liberty constraints are asymmetric.** Giving up freedom to prevent a speculative harm is a one-way door.
