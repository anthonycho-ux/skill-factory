# /character-360 — The Realization Test

## What It Is

Before committing to a character — in writing, game design, role-playing, or AI persona development — apply the 360-degree test: can you predict how this character would behave in scenarios you haven't written yet?

Dan Houser method: live with a character in your head until you know them better than any scenario requires. If you can't predict them, they aren't fully realized yet.

---

## Trigger

Invoke when:
- "I need to develop a character"
- "Who is [character] really?"
- "I have a premise — how do I test if the character holds?"
- "Why does this character feel thin?"
- Building an AI persona or agent character

---

## Inputs

- **Character** — name, premise, or role description
- **Scenario count** — how many scenarios to test (default: 3)

---

## Phase 1 — Seed

1. Write the character in one sentence.
   Example: "A retired gunfighter with a wife who regrets nothing."

2. Write one line of dialogue they would say unprompted.
   Not in response to anything — what do they say when they think no one is listening?

---

## Phase 2 — 360 Test

For each scenario, write one paragraph:

3. **Scenario: first thing in the morning.**
   Who are they before the world has asked anything of them?

4. **Scenario: a fight they're going to lose.**
   How do they behave when outmatched and no one can help?

5. **Scenario: a moment of unexpected kindness.**
   How do they receive something they didn't earn?

---

## Phase 3 — Diagnose

6. If the character answered each scenario consistently and specifically → they hold.
7. If the character gave vague, generic, or contradictory answers → they are a position, not a person. Return to Phase 1.
8. If you couldn't write the paragraph at all → the character needs a stronger seed sentence.

---

## Success Criteria

- [ ] Seed sentence written
- [ ] Unprompted line of dialogue written
- [ ] Three scenarios answered with specific, consistent behavior
- [ ] Character holds across scenarios without contradiction

---

## Rules

- **The test is not brainstorming.** Write the scenario — don't describe what the character might do.
- **Generic answers indicate thin seeds.** "They'd probably be nervous" is not a character. "They'd be polishing their boots" is.
- **Contradictions are signal, not error.** If the character surprised you, the seed sentence is wrong — not the character.

---

## The Deeper Principle

**"I know them better than any scenario requires."** — Dan Houser

A character isn't a collection of traits. It's a specific person you can predict. When you can answer questions the scenario never posed, the character is ready.
