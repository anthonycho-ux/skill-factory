# /reality-punch — Collapse False Tradeoffs with Evidence

## What It Is

Collapse a false tradeoff framing by substituting a direct empirical counterexample. The rebuttal is not argumentative — it is evidential. You are not claiming the tradeoff is theoretically wrong; you are asserting it is historically false.

---

## Trigger

Invoke when:
- Someone presents a binary constraint as inevitable ("you must choose between X and Y")
- A policy debate frames two things as mutually exclusive
- An argument depends on a claimed trade-off that sounds like common sense
- Keywords: "tradeoff", "balance", "can't have both", "inevitable", "unfortunately", "the cost of"

---

## Inputs

- **The tradeoff** — the claim being made (X vs Y)
- **Context** — who is making the claim and why

---

## Steps

### 1. Name the tradeoff explicitly.

"So your claim is that X requires giving up Y." This neutralizes the implicit authority of the framing.

### 2. Cut with "We had both."

The rebuttal is not argumentative — it is evidential. Find a direct experience, physical observation, or lived fact that proves both were achieved simultaneously.

### 3. Deploy the sensory counterexample.

The form is always physical, sensory, first-person-accessible: a specific year, a specific place, a specific receipt. Not a citation, not a stat, not an expert. Something you can point to or stand in front of.

### 4. End the debate.

Close with a declarative. "We had both." Or "They can do it. So can we."

---

## Examples

| Claimed Tradeoff | Counterexample |
|-----------------|----------------|
| Security requires sacrificing freedom | "Moscow metro was clean, on-time, and safe — in the 1980s." |
| Immigration requires choosing merit or diversity | "Asians were getting 400 SAT points subtracted at Harvard." |
| High immigration excludes native talent | "Nobody pounded the table harder for American-born hires and high-skilled immigration simultaneously." |

---

## Success Criteria

- The tradeoff framing is named and rejected in the first sentence
- The counterexample is specific, physical, and first-person-accessible
- The rebuttal ends the debate by making the frame look silly, not by arguing through it
- Output fits in 2–4 sentences

---

## Rules

- **Do not argue through the logic.** Argument is slower than evidence.
- **The counterexample must be verifiable in person.** If you can't, find a better one.
- **If no counterexample exists:** say "That's an empirical question — go find out" rather than hedging.
- **Reserve this for when the tradeoff is a power play disguised as a constraint.** Do not use when the tradeoff is genuine.
