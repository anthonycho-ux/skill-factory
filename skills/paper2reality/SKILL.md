# /paper2reality — The Human Nature Collision Test

## What It Is

Tests whether a plan, system, or theory will survive contact with human nature.

The core lesson from planning disasters: plans that assume humans will behave as the plan requires — rather than as their incentives dictate — will fail. The failure is not in principle. It fails because it does not account for what people actually do when the plan meets reality.

> "The idea here is to make agriculture more efficient... but the implementation through collectivization had all the elements that contended with human nature. First with the kulaks, so the successful farmers were punished. And so then the incentive is not just to be a successful farmer, but to hide."

The skill finds that sentence in every plan.

---

## Trigger

Invoke when:
- User says "paper2reality", "good on paper", "human nature", "incentives"
- Evaluating any plan that requires people to behave differently than their incentives suggest
- Reviewing a proposal where the implementers are not the beneficiaries
- Before committing to any system that depends on people not optimizing for themselves

---

## Steps

```
1. Name the plan in one sentence
2. Identify who operates within it and what their incentives actually are
3. Ask: Does the plan require them to act against their incentives?
4. If yes: Is there an enforcement mechanism? What's the consequence of non-compliance?
5. Score: Paper Reality Gap
6. Apply the Ratchet Test: is there a correction mechanism?
7. Output: The Gap → The Incentives → The Failure Mode
```

## The Paper Reality Gap

**Assumption:** "Farmers will produce more if collectively organized."
**Reality:** "Farmers will hide their output if punished for being successful."

**Assumption:** "Bureaucrats will implement policy in good faith."
**Reality:** "Bureaucrats will optimize for metrics that keep them safe."

**Assumption:** "Team members will prioritize the mission."
**Reality:** "Team members will prioritize what gets them promoted."

---

## The Ratchet Test

When this plan meets resistance, what is the system's response?
- **Good systems:** Error detection → correction → learning
- **Ratchet systems:** Error detection → increased enforcement → resistance → worse error → repeat

A system without an error-correction mechanism will always escalate.

---

## The "Good On Paper" Checklist

- [ ] **Incentive alignment** — Does the plan make it in people's interest to do what the plan requires?
- [ ] **Error detection** — Is there a mechanism that tells you when the plan is not working?
- [ ] **Error correction** — Is there a mechanism that allows the plan to change?
- [ ] **Monitoring** — Can non-compliance be detected? If not, assume it happens.
- [ ] **Beneficiary alignment** — Are the people operating the plan the same people who benefit from it?

---

## The Intellectual's Trap

> "Any celebration of intellectuals as world movers is going to appeal to intellectuals."

The person who designs a system for others is always tempted to believe they understand those others better than those others understand themselves. The correction: include the people the plan affects in the planning — as co-designers with veto over their own incentive structures.

---

## Rules

- **Human nature always wins the collision.** Not because humans are bad — because they optimize for what they actually want.
- **The incentive is the behavior.** Ask what people are rewarded for, not what they are told to do.
- **Non-compliance that can't be detected is non-compliance that will happen.**
- **The ratchet goes one way without a correction mechanism.**
- **The person who draws the map is not the person who lives in the territory.**
