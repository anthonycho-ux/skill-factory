# /civility-drill — Treat Civility as a Trained Skill, Not a Natural Disposition

## What It Is

Before assuming good faith or blaming cooperation failures on bad actors: is civility actually the natural state here? Humans are civil, not good. Civility is trained behavior, not a baseline.

When designing teams, systems, or cultures: do not design for the human who is good by nature. Design for the human who must be trained to be civil and can lapse at any moment.

---

## Trigger

Invoke when:
- A team or relationship failed because someone "should have known better"
- A policy assumes people will cooperate in good faith without enforcement
- Someone uses "human nature" as an explanation for cooperation failures
- Designing accountability structures, cultural norms, or governance
- Keywords: "good faith", "should know better", "human nature", "trust", "culture", "norms"

---

## Steps

### 1. Name the training required.

What specific training is required to produce the civility this system assumes? Civility is a stack of trained behaviors: not interrupting, not taking credit, not forming cliques, not escalating conflict. Each element must be explicitly taught, modeled, and reinforced.

If the system assumes civility without specifying what it consists of, civility is not the baseline — its absence is.

### 2. Ask whether the training is present.

The default is that it is not. The gap between "civility is expected" and "civility has been trained" is where failures live.

### 3. Map the outgroup formation risk.

> "How quickly and easily it is for human beings to form outgroups and to just rid others, completely out of the human race."

Outgroup formation is faster than reasoning. Design systems that account for this. Ask: given this system, what are the cheapest and fastest ways an outgroup could form? What happens when it does?

### 4. Apply the Dennis Rodman test.

> "90% of the time, they will then start telling me everything they know about North Korea. And half of what you're saying is not accurate either."

When someone offers their opinion on a subject with a single obvious entry point, assume they know one fact and nothing more. The social pressure to appear informed is stronger than the drive to become informed.

### 5. Build the verification mechanism.

Civility without verification is not trust — it is exposure. Normalization without verification is reconnaissance. Partnerships without verification are options for extraction.

---

## Rules

- **Do not confuse civility with goodness.** Civility is trained behavior; goodness is what happens when the training holds under pressure.
- **Do not assume good faith.** Extend trust provisionally, verify continuously.
- **Outgroup formation is not a failure of culture.** It is the default. Culture is the set of practices that counteract the default.
- **The Dennis Rodman test works in reverse.** If someone has deep expertise, they will not lead with the obvious fact.
