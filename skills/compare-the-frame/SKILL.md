# /compare-the-frame — Frame Inversion via Structural Comparison

## What It Is

When facing a contested principle or a "radical" label, do not argue through the content — deploy a structural comparison that inverts the frame. The comparison ships where the argument stalls.

Not a rhetorical trick. An epistemology shortcut: if the comparison is real and the framing is symmetrical, the frame does the work without requiring a proof obligation.

---

## Trigger

Invoke when:
- Someone says a position is "radical" or "extreme"
- A principle is stated without a comparison case
- A contested claim can be tested by placing it next to a similar-but-opposite case
- Keywords: "radical", "extreme", "principle", "that's not how it works", "if you really believe", "what about"

---

## Inputs

- **The contested claim or label** — what is being asserted or what you've been called
- **The audience** — who needs to see the inversion

---

## Steps

### 1. Name the contested frame.

"So your position is that X is radical." Use their exact words.

### 2. Find the comparison that inverts it.

**The Robert George move** — test a principle against its most favorable counterexample:
If your principle on X would produce a conclusion you reject when applied to Y — where Y is a case you care about — the principle fails. The comparison closes the escape hatch.

**The international normalcy move** — test a domestic policy against what every comparable country does:
Every wealthy country does X. Therefore X is not radical — it is normal. The radical position is being the outlier.

### 3. Ship the comparison, not the argument.

State it as a fact. "Every other major country has universal healthcare." Let the audience draw the conclusion — they own it.

### 4. End the debate.

The frame inversion is complete when the label has transferred. The other side now carries the burden of explaining why the comparison doesn't hold.

---

## Examples

### Robert George Consistency Test (Ben Shapiro style)

**Claim to test:** Affirmative action is just.

**Comparison:** Would you apply the same principle to Asian students at Harvard — subtracting 400 SAT points because they are overrepresented? If no, the principle fails on consistency.

**What ships:** The principle survives or falls on its symmetry across cases. No argument required.

### International Normalcy Frame (Bernie Sanders style)

**Claim tested:** Medicare for all is radical.

**Comparison:** Every other major country on Earth has some form of universal healthcare — Denmark, UK, France, Germany. The US is the outlier.

**What ships:** "Radical" reattaches to the status quo. The burden flips — now the defender of the current system must explain why the rest of the developed world is wrong.

---

## Success Criteria

- [ ] Contested claim named explicitly before comparison deploys
- [ ] Comparison is real, verifiable, structurally symmetrical
- [ ] Frame has visibly transferred — "radical" now describes the status quo
- [ ] No argument was required — the comparison did the work

---

## Rules

- **The comparison must be real.** If you can't verify it in under two minutes, find a better one.
- **Do not explain the inference.** State it as fact. Unexplained comparisons feel like discovery; explained ones feel like manipulation.
- **The Robert George move requires intellectual honesty.** You must be willing to accept that the principle fails if the comparison doesn't hold.
- **This fails when the comparison isn't symmetrical.** Know the difference between a real comparison and a superficial one.
