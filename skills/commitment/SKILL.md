# /commitment — The Convenience Filter Evaluator

## What It Is

A diagnostic that identifies who a person actually is by examining their choices under cost — not what they say, but what they do when it hurts.

From Teddy Atlas: *"People disappoint you not because they want to disappoint you or let you down or betray you. They do it because it's more convenient to do than the other thing."*

This is not about judging people harshly — it's a diagnostic for predicting behavior. If someone consistently chooses convenience over commitment, they will continue to do so. You can plan accordingly.

---

## Trigger

Invoke when:
- Evaluating a partner, collaborator, or team member's reliability
- Reviewing a decision that involved choosing between effort and ease
- Assessing whether someone will follow through when stakes rise
- Deciding whether to trust someone's word vs. their track record

---

## Inputs

- **Person or decision** — someone whose behavior you're evaluating, or a specific choice they made
- **Context** — what commitment was at stake, what the cost of keeping it was

---

## The Diagnostic Questions

### 1. What was the commitment?

Name the explicit or implicit promise. "They said they'd deliver by Friday." "They said they cared about quality." "They said this was a priority." Be specific — vague commitments produce vague diagnostics.

### 2. What was the cost of keeping it?

Quantify the inconvenience. The cost doesn't have to be large — it has to be real.

### 3. What did they actually choose?

Not what they said — what did they do when the moment came?
- Honor the commitment despite the cost?
- Make an excuse that let them off the hook?
- Simply disappear and hope you wouldn't notice?
- Act like the commitment never existed?

### 4. Is this a pattern or an exception?

One convenient choice might be a crisis. Three convenient choices is a character statement. Look at the full track record.

---

## The Scoring

### Commitment Score
```
Full — honored even when costly
Partial — honored but with excuses, renegotiation, or visible resentment
Broken — made the convenient choice and rationalized it
Ghosted — commitment disappeared when the cost became real
```

### Convenience Pattern
```
One-off — unusual, explainable, unlikely to repeat
Pattern — consistent behavior across time and situation
Structural — the person's role makes convenience the rational choice
```

### The Verdict
```
✅ Trust with evidence — history of honoring costly commitments
⚠️ Trust with monitoring — pattern is good but not yet deep
🔒 Trust conditionally — requires structural changes to reduce convenience pressure
❌ Do not rely on — pattern of convenience over commitment is clear
```

---

## The Deeper Principle

**"Either you're in or you're out."** — Teddy Atlas

Half-loyal is not loyalty. A little broken is still broken. People who are genuinely committed will find a way. People who are performing commitment will find an excuse. The difference is visible in the quality of the excuse — a genuine person will own the cost; a performing person will explain it away.

---

## Rules

- **One data point is not a diagnosis.** Look for the pattern across time.
- **Crisis is different from pattern.** A genuine crisis is not the same as mild inconvenience.
- **Structural convenience is not personal.** Sometimes systems create rational incentives for broken commitments.
- **The person who explains vs. the person who owns.** When confronted, the response tells you more than the break itself.
- **Kindness is not reliability.** A warm person can still consistently choose convenience when it matters.

---

## What Gets Cut

- **"They meant well."** Intent without follow-through is not a reliable data point.
- **The explanation.** The quality of an excuse is inversely correlated with actual commitment.
- **The promise.** The action under cost is the commitment, not the promise.
- **"They'll be better next time."** Not a substitute for a track record.

---

## Language

**Avoid:** "they seem nice", "they meant well", "it was a one-time thing", "good intentions"

**Use:** "honored under cost", "chose convenience over commitment", "ghosted when it got hard", "pattern of rationalized breakage"
