# /half-life — Name the Degradation Rate Before Committing

## What It Is

Before committing to any long-duration plan, investment, or institution — ask: what is its half-life? Everything degrades. The question is at what rate and whether you've named it.

The Saylor filter: if you cannot state the half-life, you are not measuring degradation — you are assuming stability.

---

## Trigger

Invoke when:
- Evaluating a long-term commitment (institution, policy, technology, currency, relationship)
- Assessing an investment thesis with duration longer than 5 years
- Designing a system meant to outlast its creators
- Hearing "this will last" without a rate attached
- Keywords: "permanent", "sustainable", "stable", "will last", "long-term", "enduring"

---

## Inputs

- **The claim** — what is supposed to last or remain stable
- **The timeframe** — over what duration stability is asserted
- **The mechanism** — what keeps it from degrading (if anything)

---

## Steps

### 1. Name the half-life explicitly.

Ask: "What is the half-life of this thing? At what rate does it lose half its value/function/authority?"

If the person cannot answer, the claim is not false — it is unquantified. Treat unquantified longevity claims as assumptions.

### 2. Identify the degradation mechanism.

Every system has one:
- Currency → inflation (~35 years half-life at 2%/yr)
- Infrastructure → entropy (bridges, software, institutions)
- Knowledge → decay (research, skills, training)
- Relationships → drift (teams, partnerships, marriages)
- Energy sources → depletion (fossil fuels, attention)

If no mechanism is named, the system is not stable — it is merely not yet observed degrading.

### 3. Apply the George Washington test.

> "Orthodox conventional wisdom was bleed the patient to death. We bled him to death trying to help him."

Ask: Is the intervention accelerating the degradation while claiming to slow it? Good intentions are not a defense against the mechanism.

### 4. Compare to the immortal alternative.

What has 0% degradation / infinite half-life?
- Bitcoin (Saylor's answer for currency)
- Open source protocols (for software)
- First-principles knowledge (for skills)
- Constitutional constraints (for governance, with caveats)

Defending something with a known half-life against something with infinite half-life — you need a better reason than "it currently works."

### 5. Decide based on the rate, not the narrative.

- 2% inflation = 35-year currency half-life
- 7% inflation = 10-year currency half-life
- No maintenance on software = ~3–5 year relevance half-life
- No training investment = ~2-year skill half-life

Name the number. If you can't, the decision is narrative-driven — not data-driven.

---

## Success Criteria

- [ ] The half-life of the system under evaluation is stated explicitly
- [ ] The degradation mechanism is identified
- [ ] The George Washington test applied (is the fix making it worse?)
- [ ] The half-life compared to alternatives with known or infinite half-lives
- [ ] The decision accounts for degradation rate, not just current functionality

---

## Rules

- **Do not accept "it's been stable for X years" as a half-life.** That's observation, not prediction. The question is the rate, not the history.
- **Do not confuse durability with relevance.** Something can be physically durable and informationally obsolete.
- **If no half-life can be stated:** flag the assumption explicitly and decide with the uncertainty named, not hidden.
- **For personal decisions:** apply to skills, relationships, health. Same mechanism.
