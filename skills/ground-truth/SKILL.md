# /ground-truth — Return to Source When Metrics Decouple

## What It Is

When your dashboard says one thing and the people closest to the work say another, the dashboard is measuring the wrong concept — not wrong data. The metric is precise, consistent, and completely wrong about the experience. Go to the source.

---

## Trigger

Invoke when:
- A metric is being used to make a consequential decision
- Customers or users are complaining but the numbers say everything is fine
- "I know this is true because the data shows it" appears in reasoning
- A business review slide is being defended with precision about the wrong thing
- Keywords: "metrics show", "the data says", "we measured it", "by our metrics"

---

## Steps

### 1. State the metric precisely.

Not "customer satisfaction" — but "average wait time from call connection to first agent response across all calls that completed."

### 2. State what the metric is actually measuring.

Not what it claims to measure — what does it operationally capture? "Average time from call connection to first agent response" is measuring system performance for calls that were connected, not the customer experience of reaching a human.

### 3. Ask: what do the anecdotes say?

The customer complaint is not noise — it is data about a variable you are not measuring. When data and anecdotes disagree, the metric is wrong about what it claims to measure.

### 4. Go to the source.

Call the customer service number. Stand in the factory. Read the complaints. The 10-minute hold was not revealed by refining the metric — it was revealed by making a single phone call.

### 5. Fix the measurement concept, then refine the metric.

It is not enough to collect better data on the wrong variable. First establish what the right variable is. Then measure it precisely.

---

## Rules

- **Precision is not accuracy.** A precise metric measuring the wrong variable is more dangerous than no metric — it gives false confidence.
- **The anecdote is not always right.** A single complaint is noise. Systemic complaints are signal. Look for patterns, not outliers.
- **Defending a bad metric is a character problem.** The discomfort of admitting the metric was wrong is what makes it "an uncomfortable thing to do" — and what makes truth-telling a skill.
- **Direct experience is irreplaceable.** The metric gives you precision. The call gives you truth.
