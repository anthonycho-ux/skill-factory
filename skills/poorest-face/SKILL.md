# /poorest-face — The Direct-Welfare Decision Heuristic

## What It Is

A gut-check filter that replaces abstract optimization with a direct encounter with the most vulnerable person your decision affects. Named for Gandhi's test: when unsure, picture the poorest person's face and ask if this helps them.

Most decision frameworks ask "what is optimal?" — played with proxies (surveys, market data, expert opinion). The proxies stand in for welfare. The /poorest-face test is the welfare test directly: if it doesn't help the bottom, it fails — regardless of what the proxies say.

---

## Trigger

Invoke when:
- "Should we do X or Y?"
- A decision involves tradeoffs with distributional consequences
- Stress-testing a proposal against its most affected person
- Pre-mortem: "Who is the poorest person this could hurt?"

---

## Inputs

- **Options** — the choices on the table
- **Reference class** — who is "the poorest"? (Define the boundary: country, city, community)

---

## Steps

### 1. Name the reference class.

"Poorest" without a boundary is not a test — it's a sentiment. "The poorest person in India who uses this program" is a test. A global reference class is too vague to generate a face.

### 2. Picture the face.

Not a demographic. A specific face. Visit a village. Read a case study. Look at a photo. The face is not a symbol — it is a data point that no proxy can replicate.

### 3. Ask the question.

"Will this help them?"
- Yes → passes the test (but must still clear other criteria)
- No → fails regardless of other merits
- Unknown → the proposal has not been thought through at the ground level

### 4. Act on the answer.

The test has teeth only when it contradicts your other inputs. If polling says one thing and the poorest-face test says another, know which one is the proxy and which is the thing.

---

## Why It Works

Most frameworks optimize for the average or median. The average is not the bottom. Maximizing the average while the bottom shrinks is a common failure mode in policy, business, and personal decisions.

The face test works because:
1. It replaces a demographic with a person — you cannot dismiss a face the way you dismiss a statistic
2. It replaces optimization with a floor test — "does this help the lowest?" not "does this maximize?"
3. It generates direction — not just a pass/fail, but what a passing version looks like

---

## Rules

- **Name the reference class first.** "Poorest" without a boundary is not a test.
- **The face must be specific.** A demographic category is not a face.
- **One face is not a census.** The test says: pass if it helps the bottom. Not: design for the bottom exclusively.
- **Use when distributional stakes are real.** The test is blunt — applying it to decisions with uniform effects wastes its force.
- **It does not replace analysis.** It precedes it. Run your optimization above the floor.
